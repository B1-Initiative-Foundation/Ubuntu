## Daniel Alexander Payne

Daniel Alexander Payne was an influential African-American Christian leader, educator, and bishop in the African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church. He played a pivotal role in the founding and leadership of Wilberforce University, the first Black-owned and operated institution of higher learning in the United States.

Payne was born into a free Black family in Charleston, South Carolina. After the death of his parents, he was raised by his great aunt. He worked various jobs as a youth before entering the teaching profession, opening a school for Black children in 1829. However, his school was forced to close in 1835 due to a new law prohibiting the education of slaves and free Blacks in South Carolina. [1][2][5]

Payne then fled to the North, where he enrolled at the Lutheran Theological Seminary in Gettysburg, Pennsylvania from 1835 to 1837. He was ordained as the first African-American minister in the Lutheran Church in 1839. [2][5]

In 1841, Payne left the Lutheran Church and joined the AME Church, becoming part of the ministry in 1843. He served as the sixth bishop of the AME Church, overseeing its rapid expansion and the establishment of hundreds of new congregations. [2][5]

Payne played a pivotal role in the history of Wilberforce University, the first Black-owned and operated institution of higher learning in the United States. In 1863, he negotiated the purchase of the university, which had been forced to close due to the Civil War, and became its first Black president. Under his leadership, Wilberforce prospered as young African Americans sought higher education. [1][4][5]

Payne was a prolific writer, historian, and educator who worked tirelessly to improve the education and social standing of African Americans. He is remembered as one of the most influential African-American Christian leaders of the 19th century. [2][3][5]

Daniel Alexander Payne (1811-1893) was a pioneering African American leader in the Lutheran and African Methodist Episcopal (AME) churches, as well as an educator and historian.

## Personal Life
Payne was born to free Black parents in Charleston, South Carolina in 1811. After the death of his parents, he was raised by his great aunt. He received some early education at the Minors' Moralist Society School and through private tutoring. As a young man, he worked various jobs including as a shoe merchant, carpenter, and tailor before entering the teaching profession.[1][4]

## Career
In 1829, Payne opened a school for Black children in Charleston, but was forced to close it in 1835 when the South Carolina legislature passed a law restricting the education of slaves and free Blacks. Payne then left the South and enrolled at the Lutheran Theological Seminary in Gettysburg, Pennsylvania from 1835 to 1837.[1][4] 

In 1837, Payne joined the Franklin Synod of the Lutheran Church and was ordained as the first African American Lutheran minister in 1839. At his ordination, he delivered a speech titled "Slavery Brutalizes Man" in support of ending slavery.[1][4] Payne left the Lutheran Church in 1841 and joined the AME Church, becoming part of the ministry in 1843.[1][4]

Payne served as the historian for the AME Church and wrote its first history. In 1853, he was elected the sixth bishop of the AME Church. Under his leadership, the church expanded its foreign missions, reorganized its publication program, and established hundreds of new congregations.[1][4] 

In 1863, Payne became the first African American president of Wilberforce University, a historically Black college founded by the AME Church. He led the university until 1877.[1][2][4]

## Achievements
Payne was a pioneering African American leader in both the Lutheran and AME churches. He was the first African American ordained in the Lutheran Church and the first African American to preside over the Methodist Ecumenical Conference in London in 1881.[1][2][4] 

Payne was also a prominent abolitionist, working with the Philadelphia Vigilance Committee to assist fugitive slaves in escaping to Canada.[1][4] As an educator, he founded several schools for Black children and served as the first African American president of Wilberforce University.[1][2][4]

## Controversies, Myths, Misconceptions
There do not appear to be any major controversies, myths or misconceptions surrounding Daniel Alexander Payne. The sources portray him as a respected and influential African American leader in the church and education.[1][2][3][4][5]
